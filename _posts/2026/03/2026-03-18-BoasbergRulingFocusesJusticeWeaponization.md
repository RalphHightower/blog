## [Judge Boasberg’s ruling on DOJ’s Jerome Powell investigation is bigger than one case](https://www.ms.now/opinion/judge-boasberg-jerome-powell-doj-subpoena-fed-chair)

_The judge treated [Trump](https://www.donaldjtrump.com/)’s own words as evidence of motive — and may signal a broader judicial willingness to scrutinize politicized legal process._

Mar. 18, 2026, 2:00 PM EDT
By [Duncan Levin](https://www.ms.now/author/duncan-levin)

The most important part of [Chief Judge](https://www.dcd.uscourts.gov/) [James Boasberg’s ruling](https://www.ms.now/news/jerome-powell-subpoenas-quashed-trump-doj) quashing [Justice Department subpoenas](https://www.ms.now/rachel-maddow-show/maddowblog/jeanine-pirro-struggles-after-racking-up-a-series-of-embarrassing-defeats) served on the [Federal Reserve](https://www.federalreserve.gov/) was not simply that he blocked them.

It was that he refused to suspend common sense. He read the subpoenas against the public record that produced them. He took [President](https://www.whitehouse.gov/) [Donald Trump](https://www.donaldjtrump.com/) at his word. That is what made the opinion so important.

[Judge Boasberg](https://www.dcd.uscourts.gov/content/chief-judge-james-e-boasberg) did not begin with dry procedural throat-clearing. He began with [Trump](https://www.donaldjtrump.com/)’s own attacks on [Federal Reserve](https://www.federalreserve.gov/) [Chair Jerome Powell](https://www.federalreserve.gov/aboutthefed/bios/board/powell.htm) and the broader campaign of [presidential](https://www.whitehouse.gov/) and [White House](https://www.whitehouse.gov/) pressure on the [Federal Reserve](https://www.federalreserve.gov/) to cut interest rates.

For too long, courts have often maintained an artificial separation between [presidential](https://www.whitehouse.gov/) rhetoric and executive action.

He quoted [Trump](https://www.donaldjtrump.com/) calling [Powell](https://www.federalreserve.gov/aboutthefed/bios/board/powell.htm) “TOO ANGRY, TOO STUPID, & TOO POLITICAL, to have the job of Fed Chair.” He cited another post calling [Powell](https://www.federalreserve.gov/aboutthefed/bios/board/powell.htm) “one of the dumbest, and most destructive, people in Government.” He noted [Trump](https://www.donaldjtrump.com/)’s statement that “[Powell](https://www.federalreserve.gov/aboutthefed/bios/board/powell.htm)’s termination cannot come fast enough!” and his threat that if the [Fed](https://www.federalreserve.gov/) did not cut rates, “I may have to force something.”

That was not decoration; it was the architecture of the opinion. From page one, [Judge Boasberg](https://www.dcd.uscourts.gov/content/chief-judge-james-e-boasberg) made clear that motive was not some side issue here. Motive was the case. The subpoenas arose from a [Justice Department investigation](https://www.ms.now/rachel-maddow-show/maddowblog/prosecutors-open-investigation-into-fed-chair-powell-sparking-immediate-backlash) into supposed cost overruns in the [Federal Reserve](https://www.federalreserve.gov/)’s multiyear headquarters renovation project and into [Powell](https://www.federalreserve.gov/aboutthefed/bios/board/powell.htm)’s congressional testimony touching on those renovations. On paper, that was the inquiry. In reality, [Judge Boasberg](https://www.dcd.uscourts.gov/content/chief-judge-james-e-boasberg) concluded, something else was going on.

[Judge Boasberg](https://www.dcd.uscourts.gov/content/chief-judge-james-e-boasberg) wrote that there was “abundant evidence” that the dominant, if not sole, purpose of the subpoenas was to harass and pressure [Powell](https://www.federalreserve.gov/aboutthefed/bios/board/powell.htm) either to yield to the [president](https://www.whitehouse.gov/) or resign and make way for someone who would. On the other side of the scale, he said the government had offered “no evidence whatsoever” that [Powell](https://www.federalreserve.gov/aboutthefed/bios/board/powell.htm) committed any crime other than displeasing the [president](https://www.whitehouse.gov/). By the end of the opinion, that judgment hardened even further: The government had produced “essentially zero evidence” of criminality, and its stated justifications looked like “a convenient pretext” for another unstated purpose.

That is an extraordinary thing for a federal judge to say about the [Department of Justice](https://www.justice.gov/).

This was not a close call. It was not a case in which prosecutors pushed the envelope and got reined back in. It was a finding that criminal process had been used as pressure rather than law enforcement.

And the way [Judge Boasberg](https://www.dcd.uscourts.gov/content/chief-judge-james-e-boasberg) got there was the real story. He did not invent improper purpose. Rather, he looked at what was already in plain view. [Trump](https://www.donaldjtrump.com/) spent months attacking [Powell](https://www.federalreserve.gov/aboutthefed/bios/board/powell.htm), demanding lower rates and making his desired outcome unmistakable. He said, “Anybody that disagrees with me will never be the [Fed](https://www.federalreserve.gov/) [Chairman](https://www.federalreserve.gov/aboutthefed/bios/board/powell.htm)!” He said, “I want to get him out.” He said he would “love to fire his ass.” He said [Powell](https://www.federalreserve.gov/aboutthefed/bios/board/powell.htm) “should resign.” 

A [political appointee then floated](https://www.reuters.com/world/us/hassett-says-white-house-probing-fed-renovation-costs-authority-fire-powell-2025-07-13/) the [Fed](https://www.federalreserve.gov/) renovation issue as a path toward investigation and possible removal. After that, the [U.S. Attorney’s Office](https://www.justice.gov/usao-dc/) opened a criminal investigation on that very theory and served subpoenas on the [Federal Reserve](https://www.federalreserve.gov/).

[Judge Boasberg](https://www.dcd.uscourts.gov/content/chief-judge-james-e-boasberg) looked at that sequence and refused to act naive.

He was right to.

For too long, courts have often maintained an artificial separation between [presidential](https://www.whitehouse.gov/) rhetoric and executive action. The [president](https://www.whitehouse.gov/) says what he says. Prosecutors do what they do. Judges examine the narrower legal record and resist attributing too much significance to the political atmosphere outside the courthouse. But there comes a point where that posture stops looking disciplined and starts looking unserious.

From page one, [Judge Boasberg](https://www.dcd.uscourts.gov/content/chief-judge-james-e-boasberg) made clear that motive was not some side issue here. Motive was the case.

When a [president](https://www.whitehouse.gov/) has repeatedly identified the official he wants pressured or removed, made his desired outcome unmistakable and then his [Justice Department](https://www.justice.gov/) shows up with a paper-thin theory aimed at that same target, a court does not have to pretend those events are unrelated. [Judge Boasberg](https://www.dcd.uscourts.gov/content/chief-judge-james-e-boasberg)’s opinion suggested that at least some courts may be losing patience with that formalism. 

What made the opinion important was not just that [Judge Boasberg](https://www.dcd.uscourts.gov/content/chief-judge-james-e-boasberg) drew that inference here. It was that he did so openly, in a way that may signal a broader judicial willingness to read executive motive more realistically in politically saturated cases.

That is not judicial activism. It is common sense.

And [Trump](https://www.donaldjtrump.com/)’s response since the ruling only reinforced the point. In a post after the decision, [Trump](https://www.donaldjtrump.com/) attacked [Judge Boasberg](https://www.dcd.uscourts.gov/content/chief-judge-james-e-boasberg) personally, called him a “Wacky, Nasty, Crooked, and totally Out of Control Judge,” said he has been “‘after’ my people, and me, for years,” claimed the ruling had “little to do with the Law, and everything to do with Politics,” and said [Judge Boasberg](https://www.dcd.uscourts.gov/content/chief-judge-james-e-boasberg) should be removed from cases involving [Trump](https://www.donaldjtrump.com/) and his [administration](https://www.whitehouse.gov/administration/).

That mattered because it underscored the precise interpretive move [Judge Boasberg](https://www.dcd.uscourts.gov/content/chief-judge-james-e-boasberg) made in the opinion. The judge treated [Trump](https://www.donaldjtrump.com/)’s public words not as background noise, but as evidence reasonably bearing on motive and pretext. [Trump](https://www.donaldjtrump.com/)’s reaction did not undercut that reasoning. It strengthened it.

It also said something larger and more troubling about the [DOJ](https://www.justice.gov/).

The government was given the chance to substantiate its claims and chose not to. [Judge Boasberg](https://www.dcd.uscourts.gov/content/chief-judge-james-e-boasberg) was left, as he put it, with “no credible reason” to think prosecutors were investigating suspicious facts as opposed to targeting a disfavored official.

That is not just a loss.  It is a collapse of confidence.

And it matters all the more because of what a subpoena is. A subpoena is the point where political pressure becomes legal compulsion. It is the government bringing the authority of criminal process into the room.

That is why misuse of subpoena power is so dangerous. It can impose burden, stigma, cost and fear long before any indictment, and it can intimidate even when no charges are ever filed. [Judge Boasberg](https://www.dcd.uscourts.gov/content/chief-judge-james-e-boasberg) understood that. He did not treat these subpoenas as some technical skirmish over records. He treated them as part of an effort to pressure the chair of an independent central bank and, in his words, to “bulldoz[e] the Fed’s statutory independence.”

That is why this ruling matters beyond [Powell](https://www.federalreserve.gov/aboutthefed/bios/board/powell.htm) and beyond the [Federal Reserve](https://www.federalreserve.gov/).

[Judge Boasberg](https://www.dcd.uscourts.gov/content/chief-judge-james-e-boasberg) did not just quash subpoenas.

He modeled a more realistic way for courts to evaluate politically freighted exercises of state power.

And if more judges start doing the same, this opinion will be remembered as more than a rebuke in one ugly case. It will be remembered as an early sign that courts were no longer willing to separate [presidential](https://www.whitehouse.gov/) coercion from the legal machinery deployed to carry it out.
