## [How Trump’s pressure tactics against Jerome Powell backfired](https://www.ms.now/opinion/jerome-powell-federal-reserve-board-trump-warsh)

_The [president](https://www.whitehouse.gov/)’s authoritarian attempt to take control of interest rates blew up in his face._

Apr. 30, 2026, 3:07 PM EDT
By [Zeeshan Aleem](https://www.ms.now/author/zeeshan-aleem)

[Federal Reserve](https://www.federalreserve.gov/) Chair [Jerome Powell](https://www.federalreserve.gov/aboutthefed/bios/board/powell.htm)’s decision to [stay on the central bank’s board](https://www.ms.now/rachel-maddow-show/maddowblog/fed-powell-surprise-announcement-trump) as a governor once his term as chair expires on May 15 is a great example of how [President](https://www.whitehouse.gov/) [Donald Trump](https://www.donaldjtrump.com/)’s [authoritarian pressure tactics](https://www.ms.now/opinion/trump-fed-chair-jerome-powell-prosecutors) tactics can backfire.

Although [Powell](https://www.federalreserve.gov/aboutthefed/bios/board/powell.htm)’s term as chair is ending, his tenure as one of the seven governors on the board doesn’t expire until 2028. Typically, [Fed](https://www.federalreserve.gov/) chairs step down from the board altogether after their term as chair ends — the [The Wall Street Journal notes](https://www.wsj.com/livecoverage/fed-interest-rate-decision-earnings-04-29-2026/card/trump-bessent-blast-powell-s-choice-to-stay-at-the-fed-5PBRVNex6GAU9A7XZI0o) notes that “every chair for the past 75 years has left the central bank at the end of his or her leadership term.” [Powell](https://www.federalreserve.gov/aboutthefed/bios/board/powell.htm) said that he had expected to follow that tradition, but that he’s breaking the norm because of the [Justice Department](https://www.justice.gov/)’s [bogus criminal investigation](https://www.ms.now/opinion/trump-fed-chair-jerome-powell-prosecutors) of him, meant to press him to lower interest rates.

> **[Powell](https://www.federalreserve.gov/aboutthefed/bios/board/powell.htm) is showing that the [Federal Reserve](https://www.federalreserve.gov/) can withstand immense political pressure.**

“I’m literally staying because of the actions that have been taken,” [Powell said](https://www.nytimes.com/2026/04/30/business/powell-fed-trump.html) at a news conference Wednesday when he was asked whether his decision would be seen as a political act. “I have long planned to be retiring.”

[Powell](https://www.federalreserve.gov/aboutthefed/bios/board/powell.htm) said [his hand was forced](https://www.reuters.com/business/powell-says-decision-stay-fed-time-reflects-his-concern-over-legal-attacks-2026-04-29/) because the [DOJ](https://www.justice.gov/)’s probe into cost overruns tied to the [Fed](https://www.federalreserve.gov/)’s renovation of its headquarters was such a transparent bid to strong-arm him into compliance with [Trump](https://www.donaldjtrump.com/)’s demands. 

In March, [U.S. District](https://www.dcd.uscourts.gov/) [Judge James Boasberg](https://www.dcd.uscourts.gov/content/chief-judge-james-e-boasberg) blocked subpoenas issued in that criminal investigation because, he said, there was “essentially zero evidence” to suspect [Powell](https://www.federalreserve.gov/aboutthefed/bios/board/powell.htm) of a crime. “There is abundant evidence that the subpoenas’ dominant (if not sole) purpose is to harass and pressure [Powell](https://www.federalreserve.gov/aboutthefed/bios/board/powell.htm) either to yield to the [President](https://www.whitehouse.gov/) or to resign and make way for a [Fed](https://www.federalreserve.gov/) Chair who will,” [Boasberg wrote](https://www.bbc.com/news/articles/cwy6z25y7z0o) in that ruling.

Last week the [Justice Department](https://www.justice.gov/) indicated that it is dropping its investigation into [Powell](https://www.federalreserve.gov/aboutthefed/bios/board/powell.htm) but said that it could [restart the probe](https://www.nbcnews.com/business/economy/justice-dept-drops-probe-federal-reserve-powell-rcna341876), and [White House](https://www.whitehouse.gov/) press secretary Karoline Leavitt [said the investigation](https://www.nbcnews.com/business/economy/justice-dept-drops-probe-federal-reserve-powell-rcna341876) was “not necessarily dropped” but instead was “just being moved over to the [inspector general]().” [Powell](https://www.federalreserve.gov/aboutthefed/bios/board/powell.htm) [said he’s waiting](https://www.politico.com/news/2026/04/29/fed-rates-powell-warsh-inflation-00898525) until the investigation is “well and truly over, with finality and transparency.”

In his news conference, [Powell said](https://www.reuters.com/business/powell-says-decision-stay-fed-time-reflects-his-concern-over-legal-attacks-2026-04-29/), “I worry that these attacks ​are battering the institution and putting at ​risk the thing that really matters to the public, which is the ability to conduct ​monetary policy without taking into consideration ​political factors.”

[Powell](https://www.federalreserve.gov/aboutthefed/bios/board/powell.htm) is concerned with protecting the credibility of the [Fed](https://www.federalreserve.gov/). As my colleague Hayes Brown [observed in a recent column](https://www.ms.now/opinion/kevin-warsh-fed-chairman-confirmation-hearing), [Powell](https://www.federalreserve.gov/aboutthefed/bios/board/powell.htm) knows that what’s at stake is the entire reputation of the central bank:

> _Elected officials are always focused on the next campaign cycle, but central banks are meant to have a longer-range vision of the economy and monetary policy. It’s only through their independence that banks are able to credibly say that their focus is on stabilizing inflation, versus making short-term gains to please their political patrons._

By staying on, [Powell](https://www.federalreserve.gov/aboutthefed/bios/board/powell.htm) is showing that the [Federal Reserve](https://www.federalreserve.gov/) can withstand immense political pressure. He’s also denying [Trump](https://www.donaldjtrump.com/) the ability to appoint a new governor, and he could act as a counterweight if [Kevin Warsh](https://www.federalreserve.gov/aboutthefed/bios/board/warsh.htm), the likely next chair, takes actions to please the [president](https://www.whitehouse.gov/). [Trump](https://www.donaldjtrump.com/) had a minor meltdown Wednesday in response to the news that [Powell](https://www.federalreserve.gov/aboutthefed/bios/board/powell.htm) was staying on, [saying on Truth Social](https://truthsocial.com/@realDonaldTrump/posts/116490221112397902), “[Powell](https://www.federalreserve.gov/aboutthefed/bios/board/powell.htm) wants to stay at the [Fed](https://www.federalreserve.gov/) because he can’t get a job anywhere else — Nobody wants him.”

[Powell](https://www.federalreserve.gov/aboutthefed/bios/board/powell.htm)’s decision to break this norm only happened because [Trump](https://www.donaldjtrump.com/) defied norms in the first place, by trying to intimidate him. It speaks to how authoritarian pressure tactics can be counterproductive when they’re deployed against people who show resilience in the face of threats and put the public welfare before themselves. Such a concept might be alien to [Trump](https://www.donaldjtrump.com/), but that kind of character trait has been at the heart of resistance to despots throughout human history. 

[Warsh](https://www.federalreserve.gov/aboutthefed/bios/board/warsh.htm), [Trump](https://www.donaldjtrump.com/)’s pick for the new [Fed](https://www.federalreserve.gov/) chair, displayed a red flag [at his Senate hearing last week](https://www.ms.now/opinion/kevin-warsh-fed-chairman-confirmation-hearing) when he refused to straightforwardly say whether [Trump](https://www.donaldjtrump.com/) lost the [2020 election](https://www.fec.gov/resources/cms-content/documents/federalelections2020.pdf). His refusal to say [Trump](https://www.donaldjtrump.com/) lost suggested that he may be the kind of chair who succumbs to pressure from the [president](https://www.whitehouse.gov/). But [Powell](https://www.federalreserve.gov/aboutthefed/bios/board/powell.htm) has demonstrated why he shouldn’t.
