## [Jack Smith slams Trump in break from DOJ norms. It’s about time.](https://www.ms.now/opinion/jack-smith-honesty-trump-mueller-comey-doj)

_[Robert Mueller](https://www.fbi.gov/history/directors/robert-s-mueller-iii) and [Smith](https://www.justice.gov/archives/sco-smith) followed the norms they had always known. But instead of letting critics like [Trump](https://www.donaldjtrump.com/) disparage prosecutions, [DOJ](https://www.justice.gov/) officials need to speak out sooner._

May. 10, 2026, 1:03 PM EDT
By [Barbara McQuade](https://www.ms.now/author/barbara-mcquade-2)

[Jack Smith has been speaking out](https://www.ms.now/rachel-maddow-show/maddowblog/silent-no-more-jack-smith-calls-out-trumps-corrupted-justice-department). His words are welcome — even if they may be coming a little too late.

From the time he was appointed [special counsel in 2022 until his resignation in January of 2025](https://www.ms.now/top-stories/latest/jack-smith-resigns-justice-department-trump-rcna187281), [Smith](https://www.justice.gov/archives/sco-smith) conducted himself with the kind of stoic silence we expected of the [Justice Department](https://www.justice.gov/) at the time. He spoke only at formal press conferences — during which he announced criminal charges against [Donald Trump](https://www.donaldjtrump.com/) — and even then, scrupulously followed [DOJ](https://www.justice.gov/)’s guidance to discuss only facts contained within the four corners of the indictments.

His conduct mirrored that of [Robert Mueller](https://www.fbi.gov/history/directors/robert-s-mueller-iii), who was similarly silent when serving as the special counsel investigating [Russian](http://kremlin.ru/) election interference just a few years prior. In stark contrast, [Trump’s current [acting Attorney General Todd Blanche](https://www.ms.now/rachel-maddow-show/maddowblog/why-its-hardly-reassuring-to-see-todd-blanche-take-the-reins-as-the-acting-attorney-general) routinely uses television news interviews to discuss pending cases, such as the recent indictments against [former FBI Director James Comey](https://www.ms.now/deadline-white-house/deadline-legal-blog/james-comey-indicted-again-trump-doj) and [the Southern Poverty Law Center.](https://www.ms.now/deadline-white-house/deadline-legal-blog/southern-poverty-law-center-doj-arraignment-false-claims-comey).

As [Smith](https://www.justice.gov/archives/sco-smith) recently [told an audience](https://www.nytimes.com/2026/05/06/us/politics/jack-smith-justice-dept-trump.html?smid=nytcore-ios-share) in [Washington, D.C.](https://dc.gov/), “I grew up as a prosecutor in sort of the [Robert Mueller](https://www.fbi.gov/history/directors/robert-s-mueller-iii) mode of prosecutor. I speak in courtrooms. I do not speak on the courthouse steps. I don’t do media.”

But since leaving his post, [Smith](https://www.justice.gov/archives/sco-smith) has begun to let those rigid prosecutorial walls come down a little, blasting [Trump](https://www.donaldjtrump.com/)’s [Department of Justice](https://www.justice.gov/) on multiple occasions. In April at a private event hosted by the [Cosmos Club](https://www.cosmosclub.org/) in [Washington D.C.](https://dc.gov/), [Smith](https://www.justice.gov/archives/sco-smith) stated that [DOJ](https://www.justice.gov/) “targets people for criminal prosecution simply because the [president](https://www.whitehouse.gov/) doesn’t like them” and “fails to move on cases because they might uncover facts that are inconvenient to narratives the [president](https://www.whitehouse.gov/) would like to press.” Last fall, [Smith told an audience at George Mason University](https://www.npr.org/2025/09/25/nx-s1-5552723/former-special-counsel-jack-smith-warns-that-rule-of-law-is-under-attack): “My career has been about the rule of law, and I believe that today it is under attack like in no other period in our lifetimes.” And at an event in London in October of 2025, [Smith](https://www.justice.gov/archives/sco-smith) spoke out specifically against the increasing [attacks on public servants](https://www.nbcnews.com/politics/justice-department/jack-smith-speaks-trump-administration-rare-interview-rcna237688), warning that “it has a cost for our country that is incalculable.”

[Mueller](https://www.fbi.gov/history/directors/robert-s-mueller-iii) and [Smith](https://www.justice.gov/archives/sco-smith) scrupulously followed [DOJ’s press policy](https://www.justice.gov/jm/jm-1-7000-media-relations), often to a fault. They provided no updates on their work until charges were filed, consistent with [DOJ](https://www.justice.gov/)’s practice to neither confirm nor deny the existence of an investigation, though everyone in America knew their assignments as special counsels. Even after announcing charges against [Trump](https://www.donaldjtrump.com/) for election interference and unlawful retention of government documents, [Smith](https://www.justice.gov/archives/sco-smith) did not comment on the evidence or the defendant’s potential guilt, lest he taint [Trump](https://www.donaldjtrump.com/)’s due process rights to a fair trial. Then-[Attorney General Merrick Garland](https://www.justice.gov/ag/bio/attorney-general-merrick-b-garland) gave an occasional speech about his department’s approach to significant cases, but he, too, largely abided by the traditional view that saying less is more.

The conduct of these men was commendable, but perhaps unnecessary and even counterproductive. While the [Blanche](https://www.justice.gov/dag/) model arguably runs afoul of [Justice Department](https://www.justice.gov/) policy, future [DOJ](https://www.justice.gov/) officials might reconsider whether silence is as golden as was once thought. When a special counsel says nothing, others fill the vacuum with their own narratives. [Trump](https://www.donaldjtrump.com/) certainly had plenty to say about [Mueller](https://www.fbi.gov/history/directors/robert-s-mueller-iii) and [Smith](https://www.justice.gov/archives/sco-smith), constantly disparaging them and undermining confidence in their work with [labels like “hoax” and “witch hunt.”](https://www.ms.now/rachel-maddow-show/maddowblog/trump-appears-desperate-keep-jack-smiths-findings-wraps-rcna187364)

The next attorney general might be well served to reconsider the silent treatment. While it remains inappropriate to discuss ongoing investigations, [DOJ](https://www.justice.gov/) officials should consider speaking out about their work, their processes and their standards, all of which are designed to treat defendants fairly.

> While the [Blanche](https://www.justice.gov/dag/) model arguably runs afoul of [Justice Department](https://www.justice.gov/) policy, future [DOJ](https://www.justice.gov/) officials might reconsider whether silence is as golden as was once thought.

For example, [DOJ](https://www.justice.gov/) officials may find utility in making the rounds of the Sunday morning shows when a high-profile case is filed, not to opine on a defendant’s guilt, but to explain how grand juries make a finding of probable cause before returning an indictment. It would surely benefit public confidence to understand that [DOJ](https://www.justice.gov/) policy directs prosecutors to provide even more protection than the law requires, such as sharing with grand jurors any evidence that might tend to refute a defendant’s guilt.

In other contexts, officials could discuss the training and policies it uses to ensure compliance with the [Principles of Federal Prosecution](https://www.justice.gov/jm/jm-9-27000-principles-federal-prosecution), which forbid prosecutors bring cases unless they believe it probable that the evidence is sufficient to obtain and sustain a conviction. Most importantly, these principles prohibit prosecutors from making case decisions on the basis of partisan politics.

Rather than standing by in silence when critics like [Trump](https://www.donaldjtrump.com/) disparage prosecutions, officials in the next [DOJ](https://www.justice.gov/) should speak out, assuring the public that it has complied with legal standards, ethics rules and its own policies. [Mueller](https://www.fbi.gov/history/directors/robert-s-mueller-iii), [Smith](https://www.justice.gov/archives/sco-smith) and [Garland](https://www.justice.gov/ag/bio/attorney-general-merrick-b-garland) followed the norms they had always known, but new challenges require new rules.

[Barbara McQuade](https://www.ms.now/author/barbara-mcquade-2) is a former [Michigan](https://www.michigan.gov/) U.S. attorney and legal analyst.
