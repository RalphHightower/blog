## [Why Trump’s White House executive order blitz stalled out](https://www.ms.now/opinion/trump-first-year-blitzkrieg-stalled-out)

_A combination of overreach from the [administration](https://www.whitehouse.gov/admistration/), war with [Iran](https://irangov.ir/) and [president](https://www.whitehouse.gov/)ial distraction has left the [White House](https://www.whitehouse.gov/)’s lightning pace slowed to a crawl._

Jun. 21, 2026, 6:00 AM EDT
By [Hayes Brown](https://www.ms.now/author/hayes-brown)

After [President](https://www.whitehouse.gov/) [Donald Trump](https://www.donaldjtrump.com/) was inaugurated in January 2025, he [signed a series of executive orders](https://www.ms.now/opinion/msnbc-opinion/trump-white-house-executive-action-first-week-rcna189185) designed to reshape America’s political landscape. The [waves of action from the [White House](https://www.ms.now/top-stories/latest/trump-executive-orders-day-1-list-rcna188264) from the [White House](https://www.whitehouse.gov/) deluged the press, put elected [Democrats](https://www.whitehouse.gov/) off balance and left liberal groups scrambling to respond. “When you’re winning, it’s like blitzkrieg,” longtime [Trump](https://www.donaldjtrump.com/) adviser [Steve Bannon](https://warroom.org/) crowed to [The Washington Post early last year](https://www.washingtonpost.com/politics/2025/03/27/trump-executive-orders-deportations/) early last year. The [president](https://www.whitehouse.gov/)’s opponents, [Bannon](https://warroom.org/) said, were “surrendering without a fight.”

By blowing up so many norms and taking on so many battles at once, the [Trump](https://www.donaldjtrump.com/) [administration](https://www.whitehouse.gov/admistration/) created the very shrapnel that has slowed its efforts.

A year and a half later, what [seemed like a relentless flood](https://www.usatoday.com/story/news/politics/elections/2025/02/02/trump-blitz-white-house/78051061007/) from the [White House](https://www.whitehouse.gov/) has weakened to a trickle. New reporting from [The New York Times’ Maggie Haberman and Jonathan Swan](https://www.nytimes.com/2026/06/15/us/politics/trump-scharf-habeas-corpus-insurrection-act.html) shows that fears of overreach expressed inside the [administration](https://www.whitehouse.gov/admistration/) proved prescient; the multifront assault has proved vulnerable to counterattack — and in many ways it has been abandoned by the commander in chief. By blowing up so many norms and taking on so many battles at once, the [Trump](https://www.donaldjtrump.com/) [administration](https://www.whitehouse.gov/admistration/) created the very shrapnel that has slowed its efforts. The “blitzkrieg” has stalled out.

According to Haberman and Swan, at least one senior staffer worried about a [White House](https://www.whitehouse.gov/) strategy reminiscent of Silicon Valley’s “move fast and break things” ethos. [Will Scharf](https://www.linkedin.com/in/will-scharf-35214a10/), the [White House](https://www.whitehouse.gov/) staff secretary and an archconservative lawyer, reportedly had misgivings about efforts within the [administration](https://www.whitehouse.gov/admistration/) to flex its muscles to further its anti-immigration agenda. Last spring, [White House](https://www.whitehouse.gov/) deputy chief of staff Stephen Miller publicly suggested that [Trump could suspend habeas corpus](https://www.ms.now/rachel-maddow-show/maddowblog/white-houses-stephen-miller-are-actively-looking-suspending-habeas-cor-rcna205945) for migrants seeking hearings challenging their detention. Later, as protests raged against [Immigration and Customs Enforcement](https://www.ice.gov/)’s heavy-handed raids, internal debate bubbled up again over [whether to invoke the Insurrection Act](https://www.ms.now/opinion/minneapolis-trump-insurrection-act-truth-social-post-threat) to shut down the demonstrations.

In each of these moments, [Scharf](https://www.linkedin.com/in/will-scharf-35214a10/) drafted memos to [White House](https://www.whitehouse.gov/) chief of staff [Susie Wiles](https://www.linkedin.com/in/susiewiles/) explaining the ways that these aggressive moves might well backfire. ([MS NOW](https://www.ms.now/) has not independently confirmed Haberman and Swan’s reporting but has [reviewed the memos](https://static01.nyt.com/newsgraphics/documenttools/2afc51a03e41c257/7f0f0dff-full.pdf) that [the Times published](https://static01.nyt.com/newsgraphics/documenttools/ab7a26e5d4b63268/402f052f-full.pdf).) The memos themselves resemble anodyne briefing memos that any lawyer could produce for decision-makers. But the carefully worded drafts are a major departure from [the bombastic phrasing in legal filings](https://ballsandstrikes.org/law-politics/justice-department-trump-ballroom-filing/) that we’ve come to expect from this [administration](https://www.whitehouse.gov/admistration/).

[Scharf](https://www.linkedin.com/in/will-scharf-35214a10/)’s memos express concerns about, as the [Times](https://www.nytimes.com/) put it, “actions that promised [Mr. Trump](https://www.donaldjtrump.com/) quick results but kept producing costly entanglements in court.” In a memo written last October, after the [president](https://www.whitehouse.gov/) threatened to invoke the [Insurrection Act](https://uscode.house.gov/view.xhtml?path=/prelim@title10/subtitleA/part1/chapter13&edition=prelim) to deploy the [National Guard](https://www.nationalguard.mil/) to [Illinois](https://www.illinois.gov/), [Scharf](https://www.linkedin.com/in/will-scharf-35214a10/)’s warning was remarkably direct. He wrote that while the act provides broad powers to the [president](https://www.whitehouse.gov/), “it is likely that any [invocation of the Act](https://uscode.house.gov/view.xhtml?path=/prelim@title10/subtitleA/part1/chapter13&edition=prelim) would result in vigorous litigation potentially obviating any advantage to be gained in terms of the flexibility that it would provide to the [President](https://www.whitehouse.gov/).”

[Trump](https://www.donaldjtrump.com/) neither suspended habeas nor invoked the [Insurrection Act](https://uscode.house.gov/view.xhtml?path=/prelim@title10/subtitleA/part1/chapter13&edition=prelim). But time has shown that [Scharf](https://www.linkedin.com/in/will-scharf-35214a10/) and other skeptics were right to worry about how bold moves could become self-inflicted wounds. The [administration](https://www.whitehouse.gov/admistration/) already has [racked up a wild number of court losses](https://www.nytimes.com/interactive/2026/us/trump-administration-lawsuits.html) on many of its most daring gambits, and dozens of cases remain open. The departures of thousands of [experienced Justice Department lawyers](https://www.americanbar.org/advocacy/governmental_legislative_work/publications/washingtonletter/november-25-wl/outside-the-gao-1125wl/) certainly hasn’t helped matters, nor have the unconvincing slapdash efforts to [twist the law’s meaning](https://www.brennancenter.org/our-work/analysis-opinion/trumps-doubly-flawed-invasion-theory) to fit the [administration](https://www.whitehouse.gov/admistration/)’s preferred narratives.

As the unpopularity of the [Trump](https://www.donaldjtrump.com/) agenda grew, already bumpy terrain became a major slog, especially after the [president](https://www.whitehouse.gov/) launched a war against [Iran](https://irangov.ir/) in late February. The conflict has theoretically ended, but there are no details on what, if anything, has been gained, aside from a clear legacy of higher prices. Focus on the war has likewise [sapped any political capital](https://www.ms.now/all-in/trump-iran-war-economy-midterms-republicans-democrats-senate-control) that the [White House](https://www.whitehouse.gov/) may have hoped to spend in preserving [Republican](https://www.gop.com/) majorities in [Congress](https://www.congress.gov/) in this fall’s midterms.

Any remaining momentum in reshaping the government has ground to a near halt with the [president](https://www.whitehouse.gov/)’s focus on his own vanity projects.

Any remaining momentum in reshaping the government has ground to a near halt with the [president](https://www.whitehouse.gov/)’s [focus on his own vanity projects](https://www.ms.now/opinion/trump-250-anniversary-bill-monuments). Although they’re still corrupt overreaches in their own way, [Trump](https://www.donaldjtrump.com/)’s plans to gild equine statues around [Washington](https://dc.gov/) and paint the bottom of the reflecting pool blue don’t rise to the same level as deploying the [U.S. Army](https://www.army.mil/) against protesters. As [my colleague Zeeshan Aleem](https://www.ms.now/opinion/trump-ufc-fight-white-house-populism) recently noted, “having started a disastrous, failed war of choice and holding record-low approval numbers, [Trump](https://www.donaldjtrump.com/) has retreated to party planning and interior decorating.”

There are clearly portions of the initial “flood the zone” agenda that are still chugging forward. Mass deportation efforts remain ongoing in the background, though [ICE](https://www.ice.gov/) has yet to release updated arrest data since scaling back its more attention-grabbing operations in late January. Likewise, the [administration](https://www.whitehouse.gov/admistration/) continues to undercut [Congress](https://www.congress.gov/) and wage [Trump](https://www.donaldjtrump.com/)’s retribution campaign against his enemies. Federal departments and agencies out of step with the [MAGA](https://www.donaldjtrump.com/) ethos remain under threat, as new efforts to [downsize the Education Department](https://www.wral.com/news/ap/34834-trump-moves-oversight-of-special-education-and-civil-rights-from-the-education-department/) Tuesday show. And [the Supreme Court is still due to weigh in](https://thehill.com/regulation/court-battles/5922350-supreme-court-final-opinions-trump-agenda/) on several cases that could further empower the executive branch.

But the radical period of transformation that those around [Trump](https://www.donaldjtrump.com/) hoped to foster has not come to pass. There has been simply too much resistance for the [administration](https://www.whitehouse.gov/admistration/) to fully overwhelm the system. There is no time for [Trump](https://www.donaldjtrump.com/)’s foes to let down their defenses, however. The retrenchment we’re seeing from the [White House](https://www.whitehouse.gov/) ahead of the midterms could precipitate a renewed campaign next year. But rather than simply accept the oncoming storm as inevitable, there is still time to ensure that in the long term [Trump](https://www.donaldjtrump.com/)’s sound and fury signify nothing.
