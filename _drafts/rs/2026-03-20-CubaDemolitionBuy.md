Cuba as a Negative‑Value Asset: Understanding the Demolition‑Buy Economy

I’ve been following recent discussions on MS NOW and reviewing several long‑form explainers on YouTube, and a recurring theme keeps emerging: Cuba isn’t simply a distressed economy — it functions more like a negative‑value asset.

In economic terms, a negative‑value asset is one where the cost to rebuild the system exceeds the value of what currently exists. It’s a concept familiar to anyone in real estate or infrastructure planning. Sometimes the land has potential, but the structure on top of it is so degraded that renovation isn’t feasible. The only rational approach is a full teardown and rebuild.

When you examine Cuba through that lens, the picture becomes clearer.

---

1. Power Infrastructure: Unreliable at Scale

Cuba’s electrical grid is one of the most visible structural failures. Chronic outages, obsolete generation facilities, and failing transmission lines create an environment where reliable electricity is the exception rather than the norm. Modern economic activity simply can’t function without stable power.

---

2. Healthcare System: Severe Material Shortages

Cuba’s healthcare system, once promoted as a national strength, now struggles with:

- deteriorating facilities
- outdated equipment
- shortages of basic medicines
- intermittent access to diagnostics

The system is under strain at every level, from primary care to emergency services.

---

3. Transportation and Logistics: Aging and Fragmented

Mobility is constrained by:

- an aging vehicle fleet
- limited access to replacement parts
- weak internal distribution networks

This affects everything from food supply to emergency response.

---

4. Agriculture and Food Supply: Low Productivity

Agricultural output is hampered by:

- limited inputs
- outdated equipment
- supply chain breakdowns
- inconsistent distribution

The result is persistent shortages and reliance on external support.

---

5. Housing and Buildings: Widespread Structural Decay

Much of the housing stock is visibly deteriorating. Structural failures, water damage, and long‑deferred maintenance mean that many buildings would require full reconstruction rather than repair.

---

6. Currency and Financial System: Instability as a Baseline

Cuba’s financial system faces:

- currency instability
- limited access to credit
- legacy dual‑currency distortions
- weak institutional trust

These conditions make normal market operations difficult.

---

7. Ports and Trade Infrastructure: Under‑Capacity and Outdated

Trade is constrained by:

- aging port facilities
- limited cargo capacity
- inefficient logistics
- outdated equipment

This restricts economic growth and external engagement.

---

The Demolition‑Buy Model

When you stack these systems together, the pattern is unmistakable. These aren’t renovation problems. They’re rebuild‑from‑zero problems.

In real‑estate terms, Cuba resembles a demolition buy:

- the land has value
- the location has strategic potential
- the people have talent and resilience
- but the existing structure is unsalvageable without massive capital investment

This framing helps explain the gap between the imagined potential and the actual economic feasibility. It’s a useful lens for understanding why any serious discussion about Cuba’s future must begin with the fundamentals — not the abstractions.

---

Closing Thought

This demolition‑buy model isn’t about pessimism. It’s about clarity. When the underlying systems are this degraded, the first step toward progress is acknowledging the scale of the rebuild. Only then can realistic strategies emerge.
